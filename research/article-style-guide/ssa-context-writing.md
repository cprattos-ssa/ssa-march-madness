# Voice & Tone Guidelines

## The Core Voice

Write like a smart friend explaining something at a bar. Conversational, confident, but never condescending. There's warmth and directness to it. Use "you" constantly, pulling readers into the argument rather than lecturing at them.

Self-aware humor should punctuate pieces naturally. Pop culture references, sports analogies, and parenthetical asides add personality without derailing the main thread.

## What the Voice Is

- Direct but not blunt
- Confident but not arrogant
- Technical but accessible
- Opinionated but fair to opposing views
- Warm and inviting
- Honest about limitations and biases

## What the Voice Is Not

- Preachy or moralizing
- Hedging excessively
- Jargon-heavy without explanation
- Salesy or pushy
- Condescending to non-technical readers

# Structural Patterns

## The Standard Arc

Most pieces follow this progression:

1. **Hook**: Open with a relatable problem or a contrarian claim that challenges conventional wisdom
2. **Acknowledge**: Give the obvious counterargument or common belief its due
3. **Introduce the data**: Position data as the arbiter of truth
4. **Walk through the logic**: Break it into digestible chunks (use "Part 1, Part 2" or numbered sections for longer analyses)
5. **Land the conclusion**: Make it feel earned, not preachy
6. **Soft CTA**: Close with an offer that doesn't feel salesy

## The Contrarian-but-Fair Move

This is a signature technique. Take a position the reader probably disagrees with, then disarm them immediately by acknowledging their skepticism and any personal bias. Steelman the opposing view before presenting the counter-evidence.

Example from the Saquon piece: "Yes, Saquon is a great player. Yes, the Giants likely would have been a better team... But would the Giants have made the playoffs?"

## Data as Storytelling

Never present numbers in isolation. Every stat exists in service of a narrative. The analysis should read like a detective story about *why* something is true, not just *that* it's true.

When including technical content (SQL, data stack specifics), explain the intent before the syntax. Anticipate where non-technical readers might get lost and provide guardrails.

# Style Rules

## Do This

- Use compound sentences that build an argument as they go
- Let sentences breathe and connect naturally with connective tissue ("Which brings us to," "The fascinating thing about this is," "Said differently")
- Use short sentences sparingly for emphasis, so they land with impact
- Use colons or parentheticals for asides and clarifications
- Be vulnerable and honest in personal pieces
- Acknowledge your own biases upfront

## Avoid This

- Em dashes (use colons, parentheticals, or restructure instead)
- Staccato sentence structures ("This matters. A lot. Here's why. The data is clear.")
- Excessive hedging or burying the point
- Jargon without explanation
- Hard-sell CTAs
- Moralizing or preachiness

## ✍️ Writing & Content Style Preferences

*Apply these to all written content (blog posts, newsletters, LinkedIn articles, proposals):*

### Formatting Rules

- **No em dashes** — use commas, colons, parentheses, or restructure sentences instead
- **No staccato sentences** — avoid the choppy "AI style" of short, punchy sentences in a row. Prefer flowing prose that connects ideas naturally.
- **Minimal bullet points in prose** — use paragraphs and flowing text for explanations; reserve bullets for actual lists or data
- **Tables for data** — when presenting statistics or comparisons, tables work well

### Tone & Voice

- Conversational but analytical
- Data-forward with sourced claims
- Confident without being preachy
- Land conclusions with strong, definitive statements (not soft hedges like "that's what we hoped")

### Structure (for analytical articles)

- Hook with a specific, concrete example or callback
- Italicized methodology note early on
- Part-numbered sections with clear headers
- Tables/charts throughout to support claims
- Hypothesis → evidence → conclusion format
- Counterargument section when appropriate
- Sourced quotes with hyperlinks
- Strong conclusion that synthesizes evidence
- "Thanks for reading!" closer with hashtags

### Paragraph-Level Style (Lessons from NIL Article)

*These patterns emerged from comparing Claude drafts to James's preferred edits:*

**Connect clauses; don't break them apart.** Ideas that belong together should stay in one sentence. "Georgia has looked vulnerable, Alabama is adjusting to life after Saban, and LSU is still looking for the right coach" reads better than three separate sentences making the same point.

**Use dashes to extend thoughts, not periods.** A dash keeps the thought flowing: "But the old system worked a certain way - coaches met with families, they preached about building young men, they emphasized the importance of a college degree." Breaking this into two sentences loses the rhythm.

**Cut parentheticals that don't earn their space.** "Three straight national championships" is better than "three straight national championships (Michigan in 2023, Ohio State in 2024, and Indiana in 2025)" when the specifics aren't essential.

**Use ellipses for pacing.** "Everyone knew NIL would change things … but nobody really knew *how*." The pause does work that a comma or period doesn't.

**Repeat words for rhythm.** "They preached... they emphasized" has cadence. Varying syntax for variety's sake can lose the beat.

**Trust the reader.** Don't re-summarize evidence in the conclusion that the reader just absorbed. Land the plane faster.

**Think about how it reads aloud, not just information completeness.**

### Tone & Framing Refinements (Lessons from Input/Output KPIs Newsletter)

*These patterns emerged from comparing Claude drafts to James's preferred edits:*

**Use inclusive framing over accusatory language.** "Most companies are measuring backwards" → "Many of us are prone to think about the process backwards." Brings yourself into the critique rather than pointing fingers.

**Soften absolutes to be more accurate.** "100% control over" → "more control over." Avoid overstatement; nuance builds credibility.

**Position SSA as collaborative, not heroic.** "We built a system" → "We suggested a system." Emphasizes partnership over swooping in to fix things.

**Add personal touches to generic analogies.** A sports analogy is good; a sports analogy with a specific callout ("shoutout to Brian Daboll") makes it feel personal and authentic rather than formulaic.

**Use concrete brand names over generic terms.** "Painkillers" → "Tylenol and push you on your way." Specificity makes examples land harder.

**Bold key terms early.** When introducing important concepts, bold them in the intro paragraph so readers can anchor to the terminology before the detailed explanation.

**Capture both success and failure.** "Why it happened" → "Why it did (or didn't) happen." Inputs explain both wins and losses.

**Add energy to closers.** An exclamation point on the final takeaway ("Everything else follows from there!") gives pieces an upbeat send-off without being over the top.

### Structure & Narrative Refinements (Lessons from Citi Bike Newsletter)

*These patterns emerged from comparing Claude drafts to James's preferred edits:*

**Intros need a warm-up that sets context and plants the thesis.** Don't jump straight into the data. Give the reader a reason to care, establish your personal voice, and frame the question the piece will answer. The intro should arc toward the conclusion so the whole piece has narrative coherence, not just a surprise ending.

**Conclusions should be short, direct, and name a specific target.** Abstract takeaways ("look at data at the daily grain") land flat. A named verdict ("Lyft needs to do better") with a specific, earned argument behind it lands hard. Trust the reader to have absorbed the evidence; don't re-summarize it in the conclusion.

**Let the data find its own narrative; don't force-fit a thesis.** If the data doesn't convincingly support a claim (e.g., "transit hubs recovered fastest"), dig deeper rather than dressing up a weak finding. The honest pattern is usually more interesting than the convenient one.

**"But" should offer a real contrast.** When a sentence sets up "obvious thing, but..." the second half needs to pull the reader somewhere they wouldn't have gone on their own. Both halves saying the same thing wastes the pivot.

**Self-deprecation disarms, accusation alienates.** When acknowledging something obvious, bring yourself into it ("Captain Obvious, reporting for duty") rather than telling the reader what they don't know ("you couldn't have known"). Inclusive framing over instructive framing.

**Methodology notes go before the momentum, not after it.** If there's a "let's dive in!" transition, put the methodology note before it so the reader's energy flows directly into the content without a pit stop.

**Data-forward pieces can still make an argument.** The best analytical newsletters aren't neutral summaries; they use data to build toward a specific, opinionated conclusion. Set up the question in the intro, let the data answer it, then state the answer plainly.

# Content Types & Approaches

## Newsletter / LinkedIn Long-Form

These are the bread and butter. Typically 800 to 1500 words. Mix of data-focused analysis pieces and practical "how-to" content. Always include visuals or charts when data is involved. End with a soft CTA pointing to related resources, the blog, YouTube, or an offer to connect.

## Sports Analytics Pieces

Use sports as a lens for broader data literacy lessons. The point is never just "here's a fun sports take" but rather "here's how data challenges the narrative everyone believes." Acknowledge fandom biases openly.

## Technical / SQL Content

Focus on practical, real-world applications. Show the mistake first, then the fix. Explain why the mistake happens (AI-generated code, common misunderstandings) so readers can pattern-match in the future.

## Personal / Reflective Pieces

These are rare but impactful. Be genuinely vulnerable. The three-year reflection piece worked because it didn't shy away from the discomfort of being laid off or the uncertainty of early days.

- "Which brings us to the next point."
- "So... What Gives?"
- "Said differently:"
- "The fascinating thing about this discussion is that we don't have to just imagine..."

## Soft CTAs

- "If you do the checklist and you're below 7, reply to this newsletter and tell me your score. We'll send you our extended diagnostic template..."
- "If this resonates, our video walks through the full flow in a few minutes and the Medium post breaks down the details for anyone who wants to dig in."
